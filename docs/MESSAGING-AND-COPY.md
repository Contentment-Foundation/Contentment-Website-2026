## 1. The Big Idea (north star)

Teacher wellbeing is the antidote to the problems we are seeing in the world.

The people who shape a child most are their parents and their teachers. A teacher is where a child learns confidence, values, and how to see the world. Climate, equity, poverty, kindness: it takes one caring teacher to change a child's trajectory. But a teacher can only do that when they have tended to their own sense of enoughness, safety, and wellbeing.

**Teacher wellbeing is not an extra benefit. It is the foundation on which future generations are built.**

> *"A large number of the environmental, social, and health problems humans face today are root symptoms of a society experiencing increased un-wellness inside."* — Dan

**The test for every page:** if a section does not serve the Big Idea, cut it.

## 3. The belief journey

The foundation of the entire site. Five steps written in the visitor's inner voice. It begins inside what the visitor already cares about and includes the trust step between belief and action.

### Step 1 · It starts with a teacher

> "One caring teacher changes everything. I remember mine."

The visitor arrives caring about something: their child, the world, the future, or their own exhaustion. We meet them there with hope, not crisis. One caring adult changes a life and shapes how a child sees the world. Everyone has lived this. Begin in their memory, not our mission.

### Step 2 · And teachers are running on empty

> "The people I'm counting on to shape the future are barely holding on. Everything I care about is at risk."

Now the stakes land, because the visitor already feels what a teacher is worth. Burnout is not a teacher problem. It is a children problem, a community problem, a future problem. It affects everyone.

### Step 3 · Wellbeing is the antidote, and it works

> "This is not a nice idea. It is proven, and our organization actually delivers it."

The trust step. The Big Idea becomes credible through evidence: Harvard research, 86% outcome held across every region, 90% school renewal in Hawaiʻi, Bhutan weaving the Four Pillars into national strategy, and named teachers whose lives were changed. Without this step, the ask in Step 4 feels like charity. With it, the ask feels like joining something that works and is gaining momentum.

### Step 4 · There is a seat for me

> "I have a seat in this movement, and $5/month (minimum) is how I show up."

The invitation. Homeroom for those who can give monthly. Opening a door at a school, sharing the movement, or attending an experience for those who cannot. Every visitor gets a seat. No one bounces for lack of money.

### Step 5 · I belong here

> "I'm part of this now. I stay, I show up, I bring others."

Conversion is not the end of the journey. Members read the newsletter, attend workshops and gatherings, share the movement, and renew month after month. This step is owned by the Events page, the member experience, donor stewardship, and every email we send.

**How to use the journey:** every page moves a visitor at least one step forward. No page tries to do all five. Section 6 assigns steps to pages.

## 4. Three audiences, three journeys

The five-step journey above is written for our primary audience. The other two audiences travel shorter paths. Know which audience your page serves before you write a word.

## 5. Every page is a front door

Most visitors will not start at the home page. They will land on Why Teacher Wellbeing from a shared link, or on Events from a social post, knowing nothing.

Every page opens with a one-line orientation for the cold visitor before doing its own job. Use the one-sentence version from Section 2 or a page-appropriate variation. A cold visitor who reads only your page's first screen should be able to tell a friend what the Contentment Foundation does and why it matters.

### First-time visitor test (run on every draft)

1. Could someone who reads only this page explain the cause to a friend?
2. Does the page assume any belief the visitor has not been given yet on this page?
3. Is there exactly one primary action, and is it obvious?

**If a draft fails any of the three, revise before handoff.**

## 9. Voice & guardrails

**Tone:** Warm, grounded, clear, human, never sales-driven. Hope leads; crisis supports. Name hard truths plainly; never shout them.

For persona depth and writing craft, see [Voice & Tone](./VOICE-AND-TONE.md). This section holds **copy-specific rules** that apply sitewide.

### Always

- Frame giving as joining a movement, taking a seat, showing up.
- Language: $5/month, monthly community, Homeroom, members, supporters.
- Concrete language at every entry and exit point (heroes, closers, share snippets). Metaphor only mid-page, where the reader is already warm.
- Local language with inline translation where it appears in source material (pilina, naʻau). It signals depth.

### Never

- "Donor" or "donors," anywhere, in any form.
- Em dashes. Use commas, colons, or periods.
- The words "quiet" and "steady."
- "Upstream" or "upstream cause." Use "antidote" or "foundation" framing instead.
- Scarcity alarms, countdown-timer urgency, guilt framing, or pity framing of teachers or students.
- Describing the work as "program rollout," "intervention," or "curriculum" in member-facing copy.

Teachers are heroes running on empty, never victims.
